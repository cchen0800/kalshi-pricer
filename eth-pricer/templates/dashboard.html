How this thing works

A probabilistic pricing engine for Kalshi hourly ETH contracts. It computes a settlement-aware fair value for every strike, compares it to the market price, and surfaces the gap as edge. Two automated bots — selective and aggressive — trade different sides of that edge under different regime conditions.

The contract

Kalshi runs ~190 binary contracts per hour on ETH, each at a different strike:

"Will ETH be above $2,400 at 6:00 PM today?"
If yes → the contract pays $1. If no → it pays $0.

The price floats between 1¢ and 99¢ and reads directly as a probability: YES at 70¢ ≈ 70% implied chance. Every YES has a matching NO at ~(100 − YES)¢, since exactly one side wins.

The probability model

The model computes P(settlement average > strike) for each contract. It uses three inputs:

  1. Realized volatility (σ) — Yang-Zhang estimator over the last 60 one-minute candles from Coinbase. Expressed as annualized percent. This is the "jumpiness" of ETH right now.
  2. Time to settlement — seconds remaining until the averaging window closes.
  3. Distance from spot — how far the strike is from the current Coinbase price.

Given those three, the model asks: "If ETH keeps random-walking at this volatility with zero directional drift, what fraction of paths produce a settlement average above the strike?"

Path-dependent settlement pricing

This is where it gets interesting. Kalshi doesn't settle on a point-in-time price — it settles on the simple average of the ERTI rate over the final 60 seconds (the "averaging window"). This averaging has two consequences the model explicitly handles:

Outside the window (more than 60s left):
The averaged settlement has variance σ²·(T − 2W/3), not the usual σ²·T. The 2W/3 reduction comes from the smoothing effect of averaging — it shaves ~67% of endpoint variance at T = 60s and ~13% at T = 5 min. Deep OTM strikes become less likely to pay out than a naive model would suggest.
Inside the window (under 60s left):
Part of the average is already locked in from observed ticks. The model splits the mean into w_r·realized_avg + w_n·spot where w_r is the fraction of the window already elapsed. The remaining variance is only σ²·T/3 — one-third of what you'd expect from an endpoint reading, because the time-average of a Brownian path has much less variance than its endpoint.

The math is arithmetic Brownian motion on absolute prices — exact at ETH scales where σ·√(60s) ≈ 0.13%. No drift, no mean-reversion, no directional view. The model is intentionally direction-agnostic: it only has an opinion on how big moves can be, never which way.

Isotonic calibration

The raw model probability is then passed through an isotonic regression calibrator fit on historical polls joined against settlements. The raw model tends to overpredict in the mid-band (e.g., predicting ~24% where the realized rate is ~15%). The calibrator is a monotone piecewise-linear map that corrects this bias without changing the pricer math. It's fit offline and loaded at startup from calibrator.json.

The buy gate (below) operates on the calibrated probability, not the raw one — so the calibrator directly influences which trades get taken.

The buy gate

Having positive edge isn't enough to trade. The engine applies a set of regime gates to every potential BUY entry — conditions that must all be true before an order is eligible. These were calibrated from bucketed backtests of settled events.

GateBUY YESBUY NO
Volatility floor (σ)≥ 50% annualized≥ 20% annualized
Distance from spot|strike − spot| / spot ≥ 0.10%
Time remaining≥ 15 minutes to settlement
Calibrated probability band5% ≤ model_prob < 85%
Net edge after fees≥ 3.0¢ (set per bot profile)

Why each gate exists

  • σ floor — In calm markets (σ < 20%), apparent edges come from noise in the volatility estimate, not real mispricing. The BUY YES floor is higher (50%) because YES-side alpha concentrates at high vol; BUY NO alpha exists across a wider vol range per bucketed backtests.
  • Distance from spot — ATM strikes are efficiently priced. Edge only appears when the strike is far enough from spot that the market underestimates tail probability.
  • Time remaining — Below 15 minutes, the averaging-window variance collapse makes the model's edge estimates noisier and order-placement latency becomes significant.
  • Probability band — Outside 5%–85%, the calibrator is fit on sparse tail data and the extreme probabilities are unreliable. The 1¢ tick floor also creates rounding artifacts at the extremes.
  • Net edge floor — Kalshi's taker fee varies by price (~2¢ at 50¢, ~0¢ near 0/100¢). Edge is computed after subtracting this fee, so 3¢ net means the gross edge was ~5¢ at mid-range prices.

SELLs are never gated. Closing an existing long is always allowed when the model says the book is overpaying — the executor only checks that you actually hold the position.

Why the split?

Selective (BUY NO) is the primary bot. Bucketed backtests show NO-side alpha is cleaner — shadow signal edge translates 1:1 to realized P&L (+6¢/contract predicted ≈ +6¢ realized). It works in calmer markets (σ ≥ 20%) where the market slightly overprices YES on strikes well away from spot. Think of it as betting against unlikely outcomes that the market is overvaluing.
Aggressive (BUY YES) is the high-vol bot. It only fires when ETH is moving significantly (σ ≥ 50%) and buys cheap YES contracts — essentially discounted lottery tickets on tail outcomes. When it hits, the $1 payoff on a 12¢ ticket is large. But it requires genuine volatility to be profitable; in calm markets, those cheap tickets expire worthless.

Execution guards

Beyond the buy gate, the executor enforces hardcoded safety limits that no config change can override:

  • Max 100 contracts per order — backstop; edge scaling and notional cap bind first.
  • Max 50 contracts per strike — prevents concentration in a single outcome.
  • Max 4 orders per minute, max 3 per poll cycle — rate limiting.
  • 3-second order TTL — orders are priced at the ask to take immediately. If the ask moves during placement latency, the order rests and gets adverse-selected. After 3 seconds, unfilled orders are cancelled (IOC-style). Historical data: maker fills lost money; taker fills made +83% ROI.
  • Edge-scaled sizing — position size scales logarithmically with edge, saturating at 10¢ net edge (full conviction). A 3¢ edge deploys a fraction of notional budget; a 10¢+ edge deploys the full allowed amount.

What the model doesn't know

The model is direction-agnostic. It assumes the next move is equally likely up or down — it only has a view on how big moves can be, not which way. If ETH is in a clear trend, the market prices that in but our model doesn't.

Symptom: every strike above spot flags "Buy YES" while every strike below flags "Buy NO." That's the model fighting the trend — skip those signals.

Caveats

  • Price source gap. Kalshi settles on the CF Benchmarks ERTI (average of six exchanges); we proxy with Coinbase alone. Under stress these can drift, so edges under ~$10 of ETH distance may be basis noise, not mispricing.
  • Averaging window. The model accounts for the 60-second ERTI averaging, but signals inside the final minute should still be treated cautiously — the realized-portion estimate depends on tick frequency.
  • No directional view. Deliberate. Zero-drift means we never overfit to trends — but we also miss them entirely.
  • Calibrator staleness. The isotonic calibrator is fit on historical data. If market microstructure shifts (new Kalshi fee tiers, different participant base), the calibrator may need refitting.
